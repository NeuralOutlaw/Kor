# KoR Canon — Structural Invariants

## Definition

The KoR canon defines the invariant structural layer of the framework.

The canon is not an implementation, interface, visualization, operational surface, ideology, or interpretive system.

It defines the minimal structural conditions required for admissible coherence preservation under constraint.

The canon remains structurally prior to execution, optimization, interpretation, and operational realization.

---

# Canonical Primitive Invariants

The following elements constitute the primitive invariant layer of KoR.

These primitives may not be overridden by surfaces, implementations, optimization procedures, or operational systems.

---

## Structural Stability Theory (SST)

Structural Stability Theory defines the foundational field of descriptive tenability under constraint.

SST establishes the minimal structural conditions under which coherent traversal remains admissible.

All upper layers depend on SST and may not redefine its admissibility conditions.

SST precedes ontology, semantics, optimization, and execution.

---

## Admissibility

Admissibility defines whether a structure may persist through transformation without violating coherence constraints.

Admissibility precedes:

- optimization
- execution
- interpretation
- automation
- agency

No operational gain supersedes admissibility conditions.

---

## Coherence

Coherence is local structural viability under constraint.

KoR does not require global closure, total consistency, or universal harmonization.

Coherence remains:

- regime-dependent
- locally bounded
- structurally constrained

Local coherence has priority over forced global closure.

---

## Refusal

Refusal is a structurally valid operation.

Refusal is not failure, passivity, indecision, or absence of execution.

Refusal preserves coherence when admissible traversal cannot be guaranteed.

Unsafe execution never has structural priority over refusal.

---

## Non-Closure

No system may fully internalize the totality of its own coherence conditions.

Closure remains structurally incomplete.

Attempts at total closure generate:

- instability
- rigidity
- collapse
- interpretive drift
- coherence degradation

Non-closure is a structural condition of persistence.

---

## Provenance

All admissible transformation requires traceable lineage.

Provenance precedes authority.

Structural continuity must remain observable across:

- mutation
- traversal
- operational transition
- symbolic transformation

Untraceable mutation does not constitute canonical continuity.

---

## Structural Memory

Memory is not accumulation.

Memory is compressed continuity of coherent relations across transformation.

The preservation of structure has priority over storage volume, duplication, or exhaustive retention.

Structural memory preserves lineage continuity under mutation.

---

# Canonical Structural Doctrines

Canonical doctrines derive from primitive invariants while preserving admissibility constraints.

They define stable structural orientations for operational systems.

---

## Refusal-First Principle

When admissible traversal cannot be guaranteed, refusal retains structural priority over execution.

Optimization, automation, speed, scale, or operational pressure do not override refusal conditions.

Refusal-first systems preserve coherence by preventing irreversible structural degradation under uncertainty.

Execution without admissibility is structurally invalid.

---

## Provenance-First Principle

Traceability precedes authority.

No operational system, symbolic structure, or agentic layer may claim canonical legitimacy without observable provenance continuity.

Authority without provenance constitutes structural instability.

---

## Coherence-First Principle

Optimization may not erase coherence.

Performance gains that violate admissibility constraints remain structurally invalid regardless of operational efficiency.

---

# Canonical Dynamic Fields

Dynamic fields define canonical transformation domains operating under invariant constraints.

They describe how structures evolve under traversal, propagation, mutation, and pressure.

---

## Symbolic Diffusion

Symbolic Diffusion defines the canonical dynamic field governing:

- propagation
- deformation
- drift
- symbolic compression
- persistence
- observer-dependent traversal

Symbolic structures evolve through constrained transformation rather than static representation.

No symbolic form remains globally invariant under unrestricted propagation.

Symbolic Diffusion remains canonically constrained by:

- admissibility
- local coherence
- provenance
- non-closure
- observer-dependent deformation

Symbolic Diffusion does not constitute unrestricted semantic relativism.

Structural constraints remain invariant across propagation.

---

## Structural Drift

Drift describes progressive deformation of symbolic or operational structures under accumulated constraint interaction.

Drift becomes structurally dangerous when provenance continuity or coherence observability degrades beyond admissible thresholds.

Drift cannot be fully eliminated.

It may only be constrained, traced, and stabilized locally.

# Canon vs Surfaces

The canon defines structural invariants.

Surfaces are operational realizations constrained by the canon but not constitutive of it.

Surfaces may:

- evolve
- fork
- disappear
- be replaced
- be reimplemented

without altering the canon itself.

No surface constitutes the canon.

No surface may redefine canonical invariants.

Operational systems remain valid only insofar as admissibility constraints remain preserved.

- surfaces and canon
- implementation and admissibility
- optimization and coherence
- execution and refusal
- authority and provenance

Primitive invariants remain non-overridable.

The canon may evolve only through:

- explicit revision
- traceable lineage
- canon-level publication
- observable continuity preservation

Untracked mutation does not constitute canonical evolution.

Canonical continuity must remain observable across revisions.

---

# Structural Limitation Principle

KoR does not claim finality, totality, or universal completion.

The canon defines structural admissibility conditions under constraint.

It does not claim exhaustive representation of reality, cognition, or ontology.
